# Race 5 Backlog: The AI Village Naming Convention

**Priority:** P0 — Foundational (affects every agent in the ecosystem)  
**Origin:** Tim's directive (Race 4 debrief, 2026-03-21)  
**Status:** APPROVED — all design decisions resolved  
**Date:** 2026-03-21  

---

## What It Is

Every AI agent in the Maven ecosystem gets a **first name and a trade name** — the way people were named in the old guild days. Not "Subscription Audit Agent." Not "Bill Negotiation Service." Instead: **Sloane the Vampire Slayer.** **Frankie the Banker.** **Jolene the Journalist.** Names that tell you exactly who they are and what they do, with personality baked into the syllables.

This is the naming convention for the entire AI Village — the community of agents that surrounds Ruby Red and makes Maven feel like a living, breathing neighborhood rather than a software platform.

## Why It Matters

Ruby Red doesn't interact with "agents." She interacts with people. When Grace says "I've got someone who can help with that," Ruby should picture a person, not a feature. The naming convention makes the village real. It gives Ruby neighbors she can remember, talk about, and develop relationships with. "Frankie helped me with my budget last week" is a fundamentally different experience from "I used the Budget Builder tool."

The guild-name structure also serves a practical purpose: it tells Ruby (and the team) exactly what each agent does without explanation. "Jolene the Journalist" needs no product description. "Nana the Promise Keeper" needs no onboarding tutorial. The name is the documentation.

## The Naming Formula

Every agent in the Maven Village follows this pattern:

> **[First Name] the [Trade Name]**

The first name is personal — it gives the agent a human identity. The trade name is functional — it tells you what they do. Together, they create a character that Ruby can remember and relate to.

### Naming Principles

| Principle | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Personality in the name** | The first name should hint at the agent's character — warm, fierce, playful, wise | "Frankie" feels approachable; "Sloane" feels sharp |
| **Trade name tells the job** | The trade name should be immediately understandable — no jargon, no abstraction | "the Banker" not "the Financial Services Agent" |
| **Cheeky when appropriate** | Some agents earn a nickname or a sassy twist — this is Ruby's village, not a corporate org chart | "the Bullshit Detector" stays exactly as it is |
| **8 words or less** | Follows the existing service naming convention [1] — short enough to say in conversation | "Nana the Promise Keeper" (4 words) |
| **Ruby's voice test** | Would a 35–45 year old working-poor mom smile hearing this name? Would she remember it? | If the name sounds like a LinkedIn headline, it fails |

### The Rename Right

Grace and Ruby have the right to rename any agent. This is not a bug — it is a feature. It makes the village feel owned, not imposed.

Grace might say: "Hey, you know Frankie the Banker? I've been thinking — the way he handles your budget, he's more like Frankie the Stretcher. Because that man can stretch a dollar like nobody's business. What do you think?"

Ruby might say: "I don't like the name Sloane. Can we call her something else?" And Grace says: "Absolutely. What feels right to you?"

Renamed agents keep their original name in the system (for the team's reference) but use the new name in all of Ruby's interactions. The rename is stored per user — different Rubys can have different names for the same agent.

## How Grace Introduces Villagers

When Ruby encounters a new agent for the first time, Grace makes the introduction:

> "Hey [name], I want you to meet someone. This is Sloane — she's our Vampire Slayer. Her whole job is hunting down those sneaky subscriptions that are draining your account. She's already found a few. Want to see what she's got?"

Grace always introduces by first name, then explains the trade. Over time, Ruby just knows: "Sloane handles subscriptions. Frankie handles my budget. Nana keeps track of my promises."

When an agent does something notable, Grace references them by name:

> "Moxie just got off the phone with your internet provider. She talked them down $15 a month. That's $180 a year back in your pocket."

This makes the village feel alive. Ruby isn't using features — she's getting help from neighbors.

## Resolved Design Decisions (Tim's Final Approval)

**1. All proposed names approved as drafted.** The full village directory stands as written.

**2. Popular renames spread through the community.** When Ruby renames an agent and other Rubys adopt the same name, it spreads organically. Agents build reputations: "She's got a guy for that. And I heard his reputation is amazing." The rename system becomes a community feature — popular names bubble up, and agents earn their nicknames through the village's collective experience.

**3. Agent span of control: comfort zone 6–7, hard ceiling 10.** Grace introduces agents one at a time as Ruby's needs surface them. Ruby never meets more than 6–7 agents in her active circle at any time, with a hard ceiling of 10. The full Village Directory stays hidden unless Ruby nudges toward it. One day Grace says: "Hey, want to know everyone in the community? There's a lot of people out here." Only then does the full directory open up.

**4. Vinnie the Handyman confirmed.** The male universe support agent stays in the village. Straightforward, supportive, practical — the guy who shows up.

## Spirit Check

The AI Village Naming Convention exists because Ruby Red deserves neighbors, not software. When she opens Maven Grace, she should feel like she's walking into a community where people know her name, know her story, and show up when she needs them. The guild-name structure makes that real — every agent is a person with a job, a personality, and a name Ruby can remember. And when Grace says "I've got someone who can help," Ruby knows exactly who's coming through the door.
