## Preamble: Why This Framework Exists

In 1997, the Go-playing AI AlphaGo made a move in Game 2 of its match against world champion Lee Sedol that professional commentators initially dismissed as a mistake. Move 37 placed a stone in a position that violated centuries of accumulated human intuition about the game. It looked wrong. It felt wrong. And it turned out to be a stroke of genius that redefined what was possible.

**Plain Sight Intelligence (PSI) is built on the same premise**: the most powerful innovations are not hidden in laboratories or locked behind patents. They are sitting in plain sight, waiting for someone to look at them from the right angle. They are solutions that have already been built but haven't found their biggest problem yet. They are frustrations so normalized that nobody thinks to complain. They are breakthroughs in one industry that nobody has thought to carry across the street to another.

This framework operates from **both ends simultaneously** — scanning forward from known solutions toward undiscovered problems, and scanning backward from accepted problems toward solutions that already exist elsewhere. The gap between those two directions is where Move 37 lives.

The lens through which every finding in this framework must be evaluated is **Ruby Red**: the 35–45 year old mother of two who is the Chief Financial Officer of her household, managing the gap between payday and the next crisis. She is the working poor, the unbanked and underbanked, the person making impossible decisions in the grocery line. If a solution works for her — if it genuinely reduces the cognitive load, the financial friction, and the systemic disadvantage she navigates every single day — it works for everyone. She is not a niche. She is the proof of concept.

The SIC HB1000 Solve Team's operating principle applies here without exception: **there for the grace of God go I.** Every finding is evaluated not as an abstract business opportunity but as a potential act of structural repair for someone who cannot afford for the system to keep failing her.

## Section 1: Framework Architecture

### 1.1 The Three-Category Taxonomy

Plain Sight Intelligence organizes innovation opportunities into three distinct but interconnected categories. Each category represents a different failure mode of human attention — a different reason why a powerful solution remains undeployed.

---

### Category 1 — Orphaned Solutions

**Definition:** A fix already exists, is technically proven, and is operationally viable — but it has not yet found the problem large enough to justify its adoption at scale. The solution is wandering without a home. It is waiting for a mandate, a crisis, a champion, or simply the right framing.

The canonical example is the **tethered bottle cap** — a small plastic bridge that keeps the cap physically attached to the bottle after opening, preventing it from becoming litter. The engineering concept existed for years before the European Union's Single-Use Plastics Directive made it mandatory in July 2024. The solution was not invented in response to the mandate; it was *discovered* by the mandate. The EU regulation did not create the technology — it created the problem large enough to justify the technology's deployment at scale.

Orphaned Solutions are characterized by a specific pattern: they tend to appear first in niche, high-stakes, or regulatory-driven contexts, prove themselves quietly, and then sit dormant waiting for someone to ask the question, *"Where else does this apply?"* The PSI scanner's job in this category is to act as that matchmaker — systematically identifying proven solutions and then scanning the landscape for the problem they were born to solve.

The Ruby Red implication is direct and urgent. Many solutions that could reduce the cost of being poor — automatic overdraft forgiveness triggers, portable benefits architectures, micro-insurance products — already exist in prototype or niche form. They are orphaned not because they don't work, but because no one has yet assembled the mandate, the distribution channel, or the political will to deploy them at the scale where Ruby Red lives.

---

### Category 2 — Normalized Problems

**Definition:** A frustration, inefficiency, or injustice that society has accepted so completely that it has become invisible. Nobody complains because complaining feels pointless — the problem has been reclassified, through sheer repetition, as a feature of reality rather than a failure of design.

The sociologist Diane Vaughan called a related phenomenon **"normalization of deviance"** — the gradual process by which unsafe or suboptimal practices become accepted as normal because they haven't yet produced a catastrophic failure. Normalized Problems are the consumer and civic equivalent: the food wrapper on a plane that catches the air and flies away when you open it. The hotel room where every light switch is in a different location. The parking meter that accepts only exact change. The bank overdraft fee that charges the poorest customers the most for the crime of having too little money.

These problems are not hidden. They are experienced by millions of people every day. But because they have been present for so long, and because the people most harmed by them often lack the platform or the energy to advocate for change, they persist. The PSI scanner's job in this category is to **restore visibility to the invisible** — to name the frustration, quantify its cost, and reframe it as a design failure rather than an immutable fact of life.

For Ruby Red, Normalized Problems are not minor inconveniences. They are the architecture of financial and cognitive exhaustion. The payday loan that charges 400% APR is a Normalized Problem. The check-cashing fee that takes 3% of a paycheck is a Normalized Problem. The utility deposit that requires two months of rent upfront from the person who can least afford it is a Normalized Problem. These are not accidents. They are design choices that have been normalized through decades of regulatory capture and market indifference.

---

### Category 3 — Cross-Sector Transfer

**Definition:** A solution that works brilliantly in one industry, sector, or context — but has never been deliberately applied to a completely different one. The solution is proven. The new problem is real. The gap between them is not technical; it is **imaginative**.

This is the hidden gold of the PSI framework. Cross-Sector Transfer is where Move 37 most visibly lives, because the most counterintuitive moves are always the ones that cross a boundary nobody thought to cross.

The examples are now famous precisely because they seem obvious in retrospect. Aviation safety checklists — developed to manage the catastrophic complexity of flying multi-crew aircraft — were applied to hospital surgery by Dr. Atul Gawande and the WHO, reducing surgical complications by 36% in the original study. Formula 1 pit stop choreography — developed to change four tires and refuel a car in under three seconds — was applied to patient handoffs in pediatric cardiac surgery at Great Ormond Street Hospital, reducing information omissions by 42%. The hotel room keycard that cuts all power when the guest leaves — a simple, elegant solution to energy waste in hospitality — has been slowly migrating to office buildings, but has barely touched the residential sector.

The PSI scanner's job in this category is to operate as a **cross-domain pattern matcher**: to hold a problem in one sector and systematically ask, *"Who else has solved something structurally similar, and what did they build?"* The answer is almost never in the same industry.

For Ruby Red, Cross-Sector Transfer may be the most powerful category of all. The systems that govern her financial life — banking, insurance, utilities, housing, healthcare — have largely been designed in isolation from each other and from the behavioral science, logistics, and user experience innovations that have transformed other sectors. The question is not whether better solutions exist. They do. The question is whether anyone has thought to carry them across the boundary.

---

### 1.2 The Dual-Direction Scanning Principle

Plain Sight Intelligence is explicitly designed to work **from both ends simultaneously**. This is the architectural feature that distinguishes it from conventional innovation scanning.

| Direction | Starting Point | Question Asked | Category Served |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Forward** (Solution → Problem) | A known, proven solution | "What is the biggest unsolved problem this could fix?" | Orphaned Solutions |
| **Backward** (Problem → Solution) | A known, accepted frustration | "Has anyone already solved this somewhere else?" | Normalized Problems + Cross-Sector Transfer |
| **Lateral** (Sector → Sector) | A solution in Industry A | "What problem in Industry B is structurally identical?" | Cross-Sector Transfer |

The power of working from both ends is that it eliminates the most common failure mode of innovation scanning: **confirmation bias toward novelty**. Most innovation processes look only for new things. PSI looks for the right things — and the right things are usually already built, already proven, and already sitting in plain sight.

## Section 2: The Scoring Rubric

### 2.1 Scoring Architecture

Every finding identified by the PSI scanner is scored on four dimensions, each worth a maximum of 25 points, for a total possible score of 100. The scoring is designed to be **directional rather than precise** — its purpose is to distinguish high-priority opportunities from low-priority ones, not to produce a false sense of mathematical exactitude.

**Any finding scoring above 70 points enters the Active Opportunity Queue for Learning Loop treatment.**

---

### 2.2 Dimension 1 — Impact Potential (1–25)

*How big is the problem being solved? How many people does it affect, and how severely?*

This dimension measures the **magnitude of the gap** between the current state and the improved state if the solution were deployed at scale. It considers both breadth (how many people are affected) and depth (how severely each person is affected). For the SIC HB1000 Solve Team, this dimension carries an implicit Ruby Red multiplier: problems that disproportionately affect the working poor, the unbanked, and the cognitively overloaded household CFO score higher than problems of equivalent scale that affect more privileged populations, because the cost of the problem is higher when you have fewer resources to absorb it.

| Score Range | Descriptor | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **21–25** | Transformative | Affects millions; causes measurable financial, health, or safety harm; disproportionately burdens vulnerable populations; systemic in nature |
| **16–20** | Significant | Affects hundreds of thousands; causes meaningful friction or cost; some population concentration in vulnerable groups |
| **11–15** | Moderate | Affects a defined population; causes real but manageable friction; mixed population distribution |
| **6–10** | Limited | Affects a niche population or causes minor friction; low systemic significance |
| **1–5** | Marginal | Affects very few people; primarily an inconvenience; no meaningful harm |

---

### 2.3 Dimension 2 — Transfer Feasibility (1–25)

*How hard is it to move the solution across sectors, contexts, or scales? What are the real barriers?*

This dimension measures the **practical difficulty of deployment** — not whether the solution is theoretically applicable, but whether it can actually be moved. Barriers include regulatory complexity, infrastructure requirements, stakeholder resistance, cost of adaptation, and the degree to which the solution depends on context-specific conditions that may not transfer. A high score means the transfer is relatively straightforward; a low score means significant friction stands between the idea and its deployment.

| Score Range | Descriptor | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **21–25** | Low Friction | Minimal regulatory barriers; existing infrastructure can support it; low adaptation cost; stakeholder alignment is achievable |
| **16–20** | Manageable | Some regulatory navigation required; moderate adaptation cost; stakeholder resistance is predictable and addressable |
| **11–15** | Challenging | Significant regulatory or infrastructure barriers; high adaptation cost; meaningful stakeholder resistance |
| **6–10** | Difficult | Major structural barriers; requires new infrastructure or significant regulatory change; high resistance |
| **1–5** | Prohibitive | Near-insurmountable barriers; requires systemic change before deployment is possible |

---

### 2.4 Dimension 3 — Novelty (1–25)

*Has anyone already tried this cross-application? Is the insight genuinely new, or is it already in motion?*

This dimension measures the **whitespace of the opportunity** — the degree to which this specific application of the solution is unexplored territory. A high score does not mean the solution itself is new (in fact, proven solutions score better on Transfer Feasibility); it means the *specific application being proposed* has not been attempted or has been attempted only in limited, non-scalable ways. The PSI framework is not interested in reinventing wheels; it is interested in rolling existing wheels into rooms where no one has thought to use them.

| Score Range | Descriptor | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **21–25** | Genuinely Novel | No known prior application in this sector; no active pilots or experiments; the connection has not been publicly articulated |
| **16–20** | Largely Unexplored | One or two small pilots exist but have not scaled; the connection is not widely recognized |
| **11–15** | Emerging | Several pilots exist; the idea is gaining traction but has not reached mainstream adoption |
| **6–10** | Developing | Actively being pursued by multiple players; early adopters are in market |
| **1–5** | Established | Already widely deployed; the transfer has been made and the opportunity has been captured |

---

### 2.5 Dimension 4 — Urgency (1–25)

*Is this a "why hasn't anyone done this yet?" level of obvious? Is there a forcing function — a regulatory change, a crisis, a market shift — that makes the window of opportunity narrow?*

This dimension measures the **time-sensitivity of the opportunity** and the degree to which the gap between the problem and the available solution has become embarrassingly obvious. High urgency means either that a forcing function is approaching (a regulation, a technology shift, a demographic change) or that the problem has reached a level of visibility where inaction is becoming politically or commercially untenable. It also captures the PSI framework's core emotional signal: the feeling of *"how has no one done this yet?"*

| Score Range | Descriptor | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **21–25** | Critical | A forcing function is imminent or already in motion; the window is narrow; inaction carries significant cost; the "why hasn't anyone done this?" feeling is overwhelming |
| **16–20** | High | Strong directional pressure; the problem is gaining visibility; early movers will have significant advantage |
| **11–15** | Moderate | The problem is recognized but not yet urgent; the window is open but not closing fast |
| **6–10** | Low | The problem is stable; no immediate forcing function; the opportunity is real but patient |
| **1–5** | Latent | The problem is not yet on anyone's radar; the opportunity requires significant awareness-building before it can be pursued |

A significant contributor is the loss or misplacement of child-resistant caps, which leads to either unsafe open bottles or medications left untaken. The tethered attachment principle has never been systematically applied to prescription medication packaging — particularly for the high-volume, low-margin generic medications that Ruby Red is most likely to be managing for her household.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She is managing multiple medications for herself and possibly her children. A lost cap means an unsafe bottle. A bottle she can't reopen means a missed dose. The tethered attachment principle costs pennies per unit and could eliminate both failure modes simultaneously.

#### OS-005 — Subscription Pause Mechanics Applied to Utility and Rent Payments

**The Orphaned Solution:** The subscription economy has developed a sophisticated behavioral tool: the **pause**. Netflix, gym memberships, and subscription boxes all allow customers to pause their service — maintaining the relationship, preserving the account, and preventing churn — rather than forcing a binary cancel/continue decision. The pause mechanic is proven to reduce churn by 20–40% across subscription businesses.

**The Undiscovered Problem:** Rent and utility payments have no pause mechanic. When Ruby Red has a catastrophic month — a medical bill, a car repair, a job loss — her only options are pay in full, pay late (and incur fees), or don't pay (and risk eviction or disconnection). There is no structured, pre-approved "pause" that allows her to defer one month's payment to the end of her lease or billing cycle without penalty, without a credit hit, and without the terror of a collections call. The behavioral and contractual architecture to enable this already exists in the subscription economy. It has never been applied to the two largest fixed costs in her budget.

**Ruby Red Lens:** This is the most direct possible application of the subscription pause mechanic to her reality. One pause per year on rent or utilities could be the difference between stability and a spiral.

#### NP-001 — The Overdraft Fee as a Poverty Tax

**The Normalized Problem:** Bank overdraft fees — typically $25–$35 per transaction — are charged when a customer's account balance goes negative. They are levied most frequently on the customers with the least money, at the moment when they have the least money. The average overdraft fee represents a 17,000% annualized interest rate on a typical $24 overdraft. This has been the standard practice of the U.S. banking industry for decades. It is so normalized that most people do not think of it as a design choice — they think of it as a law of nature.

**Why It's Invisible:** The fee is framed as a service ("we covered your transaction so you weren't embarrassed at the register") and buried in account agreements. The customers most harmed by it are the least likely to have the time, energy, or platform to advocate against it. And because it has been present for so long, it has been reclassified from "predatory practice" to "standard banking."

**The Available Fix:** Real-time balance alerts (already deployed by most banks), automatic small-dollar credit lines at fair rates, or simply declining the transaction rather than charging a fee — all of these exist and work. The CFPB has been moving toward limiting overdraft fees, and several banks (notably Ally, Capital One) have eliminated them entirely, with no meaningful impact on their business models.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She is the primary target of this fee. She pays it multiple times per year. It is the most direct expression of the principle that "it's expensive to be poor."

#### NP-002 — The Grocery Store Loyalty Card as a Two-Tier Pricing System

**The Normalized Problem:** Most major grocery chains operate a dual-price system: a "regular" price and a "loyalty card" price. The loyalty card price is typically 20–40% lower. The system is framed as a reward for loyal customers. In practice, it is a **penalty for customers who cannot or do not maintain a loyalty card** — which disproportionately includes elderly customers, recent immigrants, people experiencing homelessness, and anyone without a stable address or phone number. The "regular" price is not the real price; it is the poverty surcharge.

**Why It's Invisible:** The loyalty card is so ubiquitous that the two-tier pricing system has been completely normalized. Nobody thinks of the higher price as a penalty; they think of the lower price as a reward. The framing does all the work of making the injustice invisible.

**The Available Fix:** Several European grocery chains have moved to single-price models with personalized digital coupons. The behavioral economics of loyalty can be achieved without a two-tier pricing structure. The technology and the business case both exist.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She is putting items back at the register. The loyalty card discount she may or may not have remembered to bring is the difference between affording dinner and not.

#### NP-003 — The Payday Loan Rollover as a Debt Trap Architecture

**The Normalized Problem:** Payday loans — short-term, high-fee loans typically due on the borrower's next payday — carry average APRs of 400%. The business model depends on **rollover**: when the borrower cannot repay the full amount on the due date (which is the typical outcome, by design), they pay a fee to extend the loan, which generates another fee cycle. The average payday loan borrower takes out eight loans per year. The loan is not a bridge; it is a revolving door.

**Why It's Invisible:** Payday lending is legal in most U.S. states. It is marketed as a financial lifeline. The customers who use it are often in genuine crisis and have no alternative. The normalization is so complete that the industry has successfully lobbied against regulation in most jurisdictions by framing restrictions as "taking away access to credit" from people who need it.

**The Available Fix:** Employer-sponsored earned wage access (EWA) — which allows employees to access wages they have already earned before payday — eliminates the need for the payday loan entirely. EWA products exist, are growing, and have been proven to reduce payday loan usage by 70–80% among employees who have access to them. The solution is orphaned in the employer benefits ecosystem and has not yet reached the workers who need it most.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She has used a payday loan. She may be in a rollover cycle right now. EWA is the most direct structural alternative, and it requires no credit check, no interest, and no debt trap.

#### NP-004 — The Airplane Food Wrapper That Flies Away

**The Normalized Problem:** The sealed plastic wrapper on airplane food — and by extension, on any food product designed to be opened in a moving, turbulent, or space-constrained environment — is engineered for shelf life and tamper evidence, not for the conditions under which it will actually be opened. When the wrapper is torn, the loose portion catches air and lands in the aisle, on the neighboring passenger, or in the seat pocket. This has been true for decades. Nobody has redesigned it because nobody with the power to redesign it experiences it as a problem worth solving.

**Why It's Invisible:** It is a minor inconvenience for most passengers. For the person in the middle seat with a lap tray and a sleeping neighbor, it is a small humiliation. The normalization is almost perfect because the problem is so small — but it is also a perfect illustration of how design decisions made without empathy for the end user get locked in permanently.

**The Available Fix:** Tear-strip packaging with a controlled opening direction, or a tethered wrapper tab (see OS-001), would eliminate the problem entirely. The technology exists. The cost delta is negligible. The barrier is purely attentional — no one has decided this is worth fixing.

**Ruby Red Lens:** This example is included not for its direct impact on Ruby Red but as a **calibration example** — a low-stakes illustration of the PSI principle that normalized problems are not invisible because they are small, but because the people who experience them have been trained not to expect better.

#### NP-005 — The Check-Cashing Fee as a Tax on Not Having a Bank Account

**The Normalized Problem:** Check-cashing services charge 1–3% of the face value of a check to cash it for someone without a bank account. For a $1,000 paycheck, that is $10–$30 gone before the money can be spent. Annualized across a year of biweekly paychecks, a low-income worker without a bank account pays $260–$780 per year simply for the privilege of accessing their own wages. This is so normalized that it is treated as a market service rather than a structural failure.

**Why It's Invisible:** The fee is framed as a convenience. The customers paying it often have no alternative — they have been denied bank accounts due to ChexSystems records, lack of documentation, or minimum balance requirements they cannot meet. The normalization is reinforced by the physical presence of check-cashing storefronts in low-income neighborhoods, which makes the service look like infrastructure rather than exploitation.

**The Available Fix:** Second-chance banking accounts, prepaid debit cards with direct deposit, and employer-sponsored paycard programs all exist and eliminate the check-cashing fee entirely. The barrier is awareness, distribution, and the trust deficit that many unbanked individuals have toward financial institutions.

**Ruby Red Lens:** This is the "expensive to be poor" principle in its most arithmetic form. She is paying a fee to access money she already earned. The fix exists. The gap is distribution and trust.

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|

#### CST-001 — Aviation Safety Checklists → Financial Counseling Sessions

**The Source Solution:** Aviation's pre-flight and in-flight checklists were developed after a series of catastrophic crashes in the 1930s revealed that even highly trained pilots were making preventable errors under stress. The checklist does not replace expertise — it **protects expertise from the predictable failure modes of human cognition under pressure**: distraction, fatigue, assumption, and the illusion of familiarity.

**The Target Problem:** Financial counseling sessions — whether at a nonprofit credit counseling agency, a bank, or a social services office — are high-stakes, cognitively loaded interactions where both the counselor and the client are under significant stress. Critical items are routinely missed: a benefit the client qualifies for but wasn't asked about, a debt that wasn't disclosed, an upcoming payment that wasn't flagged. The consequences of these omissions are not as immediately visible as a plane crash, but they are real and cumulative.

**The Transfer:** A standardized, mandatory pre-session and post-session checklist for financial counseling — modeled explicitly on aviation's pre-flight and post-flight protocols — would systematically reduce the rate of critical omissions. It would also create a documented record of what was covered, protecting both the counselor and the client.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She is sitting across from a financial counselor for the first time in her life. She is terrified. She has forgotten half of what she meant to say. The counselor is on their fourteenth appointment of the day. A checklist protects both of them.

| Transfer Feasibility | 23 | Checklists are low-cost, low-tech, and immediately deployable; no regulatory barriers |
| Novelty | 20 | WHO surgical checklist has been discussed in social services; financial counseling application is largely unexplored |

| **Total** | **83** | **Active Opportunity Queue** |

#### CST-002 — Formula 1 Pit Stop Choreography → Food Bank Client Intake

**The Source Solution:** The Formula 1 pit stop is a masterclass in **high-stakes, time-constrained, multi-person coordination**. Every role is defined. Every movement is rehearsed. Every handoff is explicit. Information loss between team members is treated as a safety failure, not an operational inconvenience. The model was applied to pediatric cardiac surgery patient handoffs at Great Ormond Street Hospital with documented reductions in information omissions and technical errors.

**The Target Problem:** Food bank client intake — the process of registering a new client, assessing their needs, connecting them to available resources, and handing them off to the distribution team — is a multi-person, time-pressured process that is almost universally improvised. Information is lost between intake volunteers. Clients are asked the same questions multiple times. Referrals to other services are inconsistently made. The experience is often disorienting and humiliating for clients who are already in crisis.

**The Transfer:** Applying F1 pit stop choreography principles to food bank intake — defined roles, explicit handoff protocols, standardized information transfer, rehearsed coordination — would reduce client processing time, reduce information loss, and dramatically improve the client experience at the moment of maximum vulnerability.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She is at the food bank for the first time. She is ashamed. She is confused about what she needs to say and who she needs to talk to. A choreographed, respectful, efficient intake process is an act of dignity as much as an operational improvement.

#### CST-003 — Hotel Keycard Power Cutoff → Subsidized Housing Energy Management

**The Source Solution:** The hotel room keycard energy switch — which cuts all room power when the guest removes their keycard upon leaving — was developed to reduce hotel energy costs. It is simple, cheap, and effective, reducing room energy consumption by 20–44%. It has been slowly adopted in office buildings but has barely touched the residential sector.

**The Target Problem:** Subsidized housing — Section 8, public housing, and affordable housing developments — carries significant energy costs that are often borne either by the resident (creating utility burden that can exceed 30% of income) or by the housing authority (creating operating budget pressure that reduces maintenance and services). Energy waste in unoccupied units and common areas is a known, measurable problem. The keycard cutoff solution has never been systematically applied to subsidized housing, despite the fact that the resident population is exactly the one that would benefit most from reduced utility bills.

**The Transfer:** Implementing occupancy-based power management in subsidized housing — using keycard, fob, or smartphone-based occupancy detection — would reduce energy costs for residents and housing authorities simultaneously. The technology is proven, the cost is low, and the benefit is directly concentrated in the population that needs it most.

**Ruby Red Lens:** Her utility bill is one of the three things she cannot afford to let slip. A 30% reduction in her electricity bill is a material improvement in her monthly cash flow. The technology to deliver it already exists in every hotel she has ever cleaned.

#### CST-004 — Airline Frequent Flyer Tier Architecture → Public Benefits Enrollment

**The Source Solution:** Airline frequent flyer programs use a **tiered status architecture** — Silver, Gold, Platinum — to create a behavioral progression that rewards continued engagement, unlocks new benefits at each tier, and creates a powerful psychological incentive to maintain and advance status. The architecture is not just a loyalty mechanism; it is a **structured onboarding system** that guides users from basic engagement to deep integration over time.

**The Target Problem:** Public benefits enrollment in the United States is a labyrinthine, fragmented, and deeply discouraging process. Eligible individuals fail to enroll in benefits they qualify for at rates of 20–50% across major programs (SNAP, Medicaid, EITC, CHIP). The enrollment process is not designed as a progression — it is designed as a series of independent, unconnected bureaucratic hurdles. There is no architecture that rewards a person for completing one enrollment by making the next one easier, or that surfaces the next available benefit based on their demonstrated eligibility.

**The Transfer:** A tiered benefits enrollment architecture — modeled on the frequent flyer tier system — would guide eligible individuals through a structured progression: completing one enrollment unlocks guidance toward the next, with each tier representing a higher level of benefits access and financial stability. The architecture creates momentum, reduces the cognitive burden of navigating fragmented systems, and treats benefits access as a journey rather than a gauntlet.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She qualifies for SNAP, CHIP, the EITC, and a utility assistance program. She is enrolled in one of them. The enrollment process for each one is different, requires different documentation, and is administered by a different agency. A tier architecture that says "you've completed Level 1 — here's what you've unlocked and what comes next" is the difference between exhaustion and empowerment.

#### CST-005 — Video Game Save Point Architecture → Financial Recovery Planning

**The Source Solution:** Video games solved a fundamental user experience problem decades ago: how do you allow a player to take risks, fail, and try again without losing all their progress? The answer is the **save point** — a designated moment where progress is preserved, allowing the player to experiment, fail, and return to a known-good state without catastrophic loss. The save point architecture is so effective that it has become universal across gaming, and its underlying logic — **preserve progress, enable risk-taking, make failure recoverable** — is one of the most powerful behavioral design principles ever developed.

**The Target Problem:** Financial recovery — the process of rebuilding after a bankruptcy, an eviction, a medical debt crisis, or a period of unemployment — has no save point architecture. Every step forward is precarious. A single setback can erase months of progress. The credit score system, in particular, is designed in a way that makes recovery non-linear and unpredictable: a missed payment can drop a score by 100 points that took a year to build. There is no mechanism that says "you have reached a stable threshold — your progress is protected even if you have one bad month."

**The Transfer:** A financial recovery architecture modeled on video game save points would define explicit progress thresholds — milestones of financial stability — at which a person's progress is formally recognized and protected. Reaching a save point might mean: one missed payment no longer triggers a credit score drop; a utility deposit is waived; a security deposit is reduced. The architecture creates a **ratchet effect** — you can always go back to your last save point, but you can never lose more than one level of progress at a time.

**Ruby Red Lens:** She has been building her credit score for eighteen months. One medical bill sends it back to zero. The save point architecture says: you earned this. We're holding it for you. That is not just a financial product — it is a statement about human dignity.

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Impact Potential | 24 | Financial recovery failure is one of the primary drivers of persistent poverty; affects tens of millions |
| Transfer Feasibility | 17 | Requires credit bureau and lender cooperation; regulatory framework needed; fintech is beginning to explore this |
| Novelty | 23 | The explicit save point metaphor and architecture has not been applied to financial recovery design |
| Urgency | 22 | Medical debt crisis and post-pandemic financial recovery have created acute need and political attention |
| **Total** | **86** | **Active Opportunity Queue** |

## Section 4: The PSI Agent Workflow

### 4.1 Agent Architecture Overview

The Plain Sight Intelligence daily scanning agent is a **structured intelligence loop** that operates continuously, converting raw information from multiple source streams into categorized, scored, and prioritized innovation opportunities. The agent is not a search engine — it is a **pattern recognition system** trained to see what other systems are trained to ignore.

The agent operates on a 24-hour cycle with a morning synthesis output (the Daily Briefing, detailed in Section 5). It maintains three running queues: the **Active Opportunity Queue** (score 70+), the **Monitoring Queue** (score 50–69), and the **Archive** (score below 50 or already captured by another actor).

---

### 4.2 Source Streams

The agent draws from seven primary source streams, each selected for its ability to surface a different type of PSI signal.

| Stream | Source Types | PSI Signal Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory & Policy** | Federal Register, EU Official Journal, state legislative trackers, CFPB/FTC/FDA rulemaking dockets | Orphaned Solutions (mandates creating demand for existing solutions); Normalized Problems (regulatory acknowledgment of systemic failures) |
| **Academic & Research** | PubMed, SSRN, Google Scholar, NBER working papers, WHO publications | Cross-Sector Transfer (documented transfers in literature); Orphaned Solutions (proven solutions not yet deployed at scale) |
| **Patent & IP Databases** | USPTO, EPO, Google Patents | Orphaned Solutions (patents with no commercial application; solutions waiting for a problem) |
| **Consumer Complaint Data** | CFPB complaint database, BBB reports, Reddit community forums, Trustpilot, App Store reviews | Normalized Problems (high-volume complaints signal normalized frustrations) |
| **Industry & Trade Press** | Sector-specific trade publications across 12 industries (healthcare, finance, logistics, hospitality, aviation, food service, retail, housing, energy, education, technology, social services) | Cross-Sector Transfer (solutions deployed in one sector that have not crossed to another) |
| **Social Listening** | Twitter/X, Reddit, TikTok (financial stress and consumer frustration threads), community Facebook groups | Normalized Problems (real-time signal of frustrations that have not yet been formally articulated) |
| **Competitive Intelligence** | Startup databases (Crunchbase, PitchBook), accelerator cohort announcements, Y Combinator batches | Cross-Sector Transfer (startups applying solutions from one sector to another); Orphaned Solutions (solutions being commercialized in niche markets) |

---

### 4.3 The Five-Step Agent Workflow

**Step 1 — Ingestion and Triage**

The agent ingests raw signals from all seven source streams. Each signal is tagged with its source type, date, and a preliminary category flag (OS, NP, or CST). Signals that do not contain a recognizable PSI pattern are discarded. Signals that contain a potential pattern are passed to Step 2.

*Triage filter questions:*
- Does this signal describe a solution that exists but is not deployed at scale? → OS flag
- Does this signal describe a frustration that is being treated as normal? → NP flag
- Does this signal describe a solution in one sector that could apply to another? → CST flag
- Does this signal describe a problem that Ruby Red experiences directly? → Priority flag

**Step 2 — Pattern Matching and Cross-Reference**

Each flagged signal is cross-referenced against the existing PSI database to determine whether it represents a new finding or an update to an existing one. The agent applies three pattern-matching lenses:

- **Structural similarity**: Does this problem have the same underlying structure as a problem already solved elsewhere?
- **Population overlap**: Does this solution serve or could it serve the Ruby Red population?
- **Transfer gap**: Is there a documented gap between where the solution exists and where it is needed?

**Step 3 — Scoring**

Each new finding is scored on the four PSI dimensions (Impact Potential, Transfer Feasibility, Novelty, Urgency). Scores are assigned based on the scoring rubric in Section 2, with brief rationale notes for each dimension. The total score determines queue placement.

**Step 4 — Queue Management**

- Findings scoring 70+ are added to the **Active Opportunity Queue** with a full write-up.
- Findings scoring 50–69 are added to the **Monitoring Queue** with a summary note and a trigger condition (the condition under which the score would cross 70).
- Findings scoring below 50 are archived with a one-line summary.
- Existing queue items are rescored if new signals materially change any dimension.

**Step 5 — Synthesis and Briefing**

The agent produces the Daily Briefing (see Section 5) by synthesizing the day's new findings, queue movements, and any trigger conditions that have been met. The briefing is structured for rapid human review — a wisdom giant on the SIC HB1000 Solve Team should be able to read it in under ten minutes and make a clear decision about which findings warrant immediate Learning Loop treatment.

---

### 4.4 Agent Calibration Principles

The PSI agent is calibrated by three operating principles that distinguish it from generic innovation scanners:

**Principle 1 — Empathy Before Efficiency.** Every finding is evaluated first through the Ruby Red lens: does this matter to someone who cannot afford for the system to keep failing her? A finding that scores 90 on the rubric but has no relevance to the working poor is deprioritized relative to a finding that scores 75 and directly addresses a structural injustice she experiences daily.

**Principle 2 — Proven Over Novel.** The PSI agent is explicitly biased toward solutions that already work somewhere. A proven solution waiting for its biggest problem is more valuable than a novel solution that has never been tested. The agent treats "this already works in Industry X" as a signal of quality, not a reason to dismiss the finding.

**Principle 3 — The "Why Hasn't Anyone Done This?" Test.** Before any finding is added to the Active Opportunity Queue, the agent applies a final filter: does this produce the visceral reaction of *"how has no one done this yet?"* If the answer is yes, the finding is flagged for immediate attention. If the answer is no — if there is a clear, legitimate reason why the transfer hasn't been made — the agent documents that reason and adjusts the Novelty or Transfer Feasibility score accordingly.

## Section 6: Learning Loop Integration

### 6.1 The Learning Loop as the Active Opportunity Queue's Destination

The Plain Sight Intelligence framework is a **feeder system** for the Learning Loop protocol. The PSI scanner identifies, categorizes, and scores opportunities. The Learning Loop is what happens to the best ones — it is the structured process by which a raw PSI finding is transformed into a tested, refined, and actionable innovation proposal.

The threshold of 70 points is not arbitrary. It represents the minimum level of evidence that a finding is worth the investment of Learning Loop treatment: the problem is real enough, the solution is transferable enough, the opportunity is novel enough, and the urgency is sufficient to justify moving from observation to action.

---

### 6.2 The PSI-to-Learning Loop Handoff Protocol

When a finding crosses the 70-point threshold and is designated for Learning Loop treatment, the following handoff protocol is executed:

**Phase 1 — Learning Loop Intake Package**

The PSI agent prepares a structured intake package for the Learning Loop, containing:

- The full PSI finding write-up (as it appears in the Daily Briefing)
- The complete scoring breakdown with rationale
- All source signals that contributed to the finding
- A preliminary list of Wisdom Giants whose domain expertise is relevant
- A Ruby Red Relevance Statement: a one-paragraph description of how this finding affects the household CFO
- A preliminary "Move 37 Hypothesis": a one-sentence statement of why this opportunity is counterintuitive but correct

**Phase 2 — Wisdom Giant Consultation**

Before the Learning Loop begins its iterative improvement cycle, the relevant Wisdom Giants on the SIC HB1000 Solve Team are consulted on the finding. Each Wisdom Giant is asked three questions:

1. *What do I know about this problem or solution that the PSI agent doesn't know?*
2. *What is the most important barrier to this transfer that isn't visible in the data?*
3. *What would need to be true for this to work at scale for Ruby Red?*

Wisdom Giant input is incorporated into a revised scoring and a refined problem statement before the Learning Loop begins. This consultation is not optional — it is the mechanism by which the team's collective expertise is brought to bear on the raw intelligence produced by the scanning agent.

**Phase 3 — Learning Loop Execution**

The Learning Loop applies its standard iterative improvement protocol to the PSI finding, cycling through the following questions:

- *What is the best version of this solution that has ever been deployed anywhere?*
- *What failed when it was tried, and why?*
- *What would the best practice version look like if we designed it specifically for Ruby Red?*
- *What is the smallest possible test that would tell us if this works?*
- *What does success look like at six months, one year, and five years?*

Each cycle of the Learning Loop produces a refined proposal that is rescored against the PSI rubric. A finding that began as a raw observation should emerge from the Learning Loop as a structured innovation proposal with a defined pilot design, a stakeholder map, a resource estimate, and a success metric framework.

**Phase 4 — Gamification Layer**

Following the Learning Loop, a gamification layer is developed for each finding that advances to the proposal stage. The gamification layer is designed to increase adoption, engagement, and sustained behavior change among the Ruby Red population. It is not decorative — it is a core design element that makes the solution more effective for the person with the least margin for error.

**Phase 5 — Swiss-Style Presentation Output**

The final output of the Learning Loop treatment is a Swiss-style presentation — clean, precise, data-forward, and designed for decision-makers who need to act on the finding. The presentation includes the full PSI scoring, the Wisdom Giant insights, the refined proposal, the pilot design, and the Ruby Red impact statement.

---

### 6.3 The PSI-Learning Loop Feedback Cycle

The relationship between PSI and the Learning Loop is not one-directional. The Learning Loop feeds back into the PSI framework in two important ways:

**Feedback Loop 1 — Calibration Updates.** When the Learning Loop reveals that a PSI finding was over- or under-scored, the scoring rubric is updated to reflect the new information. If a finding scored 80 on Transfer Feasibility but the Learning Loop revealed a previously invisible regulatory barrier, the Transfer Feasibility rubric is updated to include that barrier as a scoring criterion.

**Feedback Loop 2 — New Finding Generation.** The Learning Loop frequently surfaces new PSI findings as a byproduct of its research. When a Wisdom Giant consultation reveals an adjacent opportunity, or when the best practice research phase identifies a solution in an unexpected sector, that finding is immediately fed back into the PSI scanning queue for categorization and scoring.

This feedback cycle is what makes the PSI framework a **living intelligence system** rather than a static database. Every Learning Loop treatment makes the scanner smarter.

Plain Sight Intelligence is a **living framework**. The scoring rubric, category definitions, and agent workflow are designed to evolve as the team learns. Every Learning Loop treatment that produces a calibration update should be reflected in a new version of this document. The version number in the header should be updated with each material change, and a changelog should be maintained at the end of the document.

### 7.2 The "Spirit Check" Protocol

Before any version update is published, a Spirit Check must be performed: does the updated framework still serve Ruby Red? Has any revision drifted away from the core purpose — finding solutions that reduce the cost of being poor? The Spirit Check is not a bureaucratic step; it is the moral anchor of the framework. If a proposed revision would make the framework more sophisticated but less empathetic, it should be rejected.

The fifteen seed examples in Section 3 are a starting inventory, not a complete catalog. The SIC HB1000 Solve Team is encouraged to add new seed examples as they are identified, following the full scoring protocol. Any member of the SIC HB1000 Solve Team can submit a PSI finding for consideration. Submissions should include a preliminary category classification, a brief description of the solution and the problem, and an initial score estimate. The PSI agent will review, formally score, and queue-place all submissions within 24 hours of receipt.

The team's Wisdom Giants are particularly encouraged to submit findings from their own domains — the places where they can see, with expert eyes, the solutions that are sitting in plain sight waiting for someone to notice them.

## Appendix C: Glossary

**Active Opportunity Queue:** The set of PSI findings scoring 70 or above, designated for Learning Loop treatment.

**Cross-Sector Transfer (CST):** A PSI category describing solutions proven in one sector that have not been applied to a structurally similar problem in another sector.

**Learning Loop:** The SIC HB1000 Solve Team's iterative improvement protocol, applied to PSI findings that have crossed the 70-point threshold.

**Monitoring Queue:** The set of PSI findings scoring 50–69, tracked for changes that might push them above the Active Opportunity Queue threshold.

**Move 37:** A metaphor derived from AlphaGo's counterintuitive but correct move in Game 2 of its 2016 match against Lee Sedol. In the PSI framework, a Move 37 finding is one that looks wrong at first glance but is correct — a solution or connection that violates conventional assumptions but produces superior outcomes.

**Normalized Problem (NP):** A PSI category describing frustrations, inefficiencies, or injustices that have been accepted as normal through repetition, making them invisible to the people with the power to fix them.

**Orphaned Solution (OS):** A PSI category describing proven solutions that have not yet found the problem large enough to justify their deployment at scale.

**Ruby Red:** The SIC HB1000 Solve Team's client-centric persona — the 35–45 year old household CFO who is working poor, unbanked or underbanked, and making impossible financial decisions every day. She is the proof of concept: if a solution works for her, it works for everyone.

**Spirit Check:** A governance protocol ensuring that framework updates have not drifted from the core purpose of serving Ruby Red.

**Wisdom Giant:** A member of the SIC HB1000 Solve Team who is a subject matter expert in a specific domain. Wisdom Giants are consulted before Learning Loop treatment of PSI findings in their domain.
