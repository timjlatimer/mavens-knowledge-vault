# Clayton Christensen — Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) Research Notes

## Core Theory (from Christensen Institute)
- JTBD is a lens that reveals the circumstances — or forces — that drive people toward and away from decisions
- Goes beyond demographics and product attributes to expose **functional, social, and emotional dimensions**
- People don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in their lives
- Key insight: "People don't want a quarter-inch drill; they want a quarter-inch hole" (Theodore Levitt, adopted by Christensen)

## The Three Dimensions of Every Job
1. **Functional** — the practical task (e.g., get to work, pay a bill, feed the family)
2. **Social** — how others perceive me (e.g., be seen as a good parent, responsible citizen)
3. **Emotional** — how I feel about it (e.g., relief from stress, sense of accomplishment)

## The JTBD Statement Format
**"When [situation], I want to [motivation/job], so I can [expected outcome]."**

Example for Ruby Red:
- "When I'm standing in the grocery line with $47 left until payday, I want to know exactly what I can afford, so I can feed my kids without the shame of putting something back."

## The Four Forces of Progress (Forces Diagram)
Two forces PROMOTE switching to a new solution:
1. **Push of the Current Situation** — pain, frustration, unmet need
2. **Pull of the New Solution** — attraction, promise, hope

Two forces RESIST switching:
3. **Anxiety of the New Solution** — fear of the unknown, learning curve
4. **Allegiance to Current Behavior** — habit, comfort, inertia

## The Milkshake Story (Christensen's Famous Example)
- Fast food chain wanted to sell more milkshakes
- Traditional research: make them thicker, add flavors, etc.
- JTBD research revealed: morning commuters "hired" milkshakes for the job of "make my boring commute more interesting and keep me full until lunch"
- The milkshake's competition wasn't other milkshakes — it was bagels, bananas, and boredom

## Competing Against Luck (2016 Book)
- Full title: "Competing Against Luck: The Story of Innovation and Customer Choice"
- Core argument: innovation isn't random — understand the job and you can predict success
- "Correlation does not reveal causality. Yet most managers have grown comfortable basing decisions on correlations."

## Cameron Herold's Vivid Vision
- A 3-year forward-looking document written as if it's already happened
- Paints a vivid picture of what the company/initiative looks, feels, and operates like
- Written in present tense: "We are..." not "We will be..."
- Aligns team, inspires stakeholders, creates clear roadmap
- Refresh every 3 years (start new one 6 months before current expires)

## SIC Integration: JTBD + Vivid Vision on Every Bingo Card
- **Vivid Vision** = the 3-year painted picture of what this initiative looks like when it's working
- **JTBD** = derived from the Vivid Vision — the specific job Ruby Red is hiring this initiative to do
- Every Bingo Card must answer:
  1. What is the Vivid Vision? (3-year picture)
  2. What is the JTBD? (the job Ruby Red hires this for)
  3. What are the functional, social, and emotional dimensions?
  4. What are the four forces at play?
  5. What is the "milkshake" — what is the REAL competition?

## JTBD Worksheet for Each Bingo Card
1. **The Job Statement**: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]"
2. **Functional Dimension**: What practical task does this solve?
3. **Social Dimension**: How does this change how Ruby Red is perceived?
4. **Emotional Dimension**: How does this make Ruby Red feel?
5. **Push**: What pain in the current situation drives the need?
6. **Pull**: What about this solution attracts Ruby Red?
7. **Anxiety**: What fears or uncertainties might prevent adoption?
8. **Allegiance**: What current habits compete with this solution?
9. **The Real Competition**: What is Ruby Red currently "hiring" instead?
10. **The Milkshake Test**: Are we selling the drill or the hole?
