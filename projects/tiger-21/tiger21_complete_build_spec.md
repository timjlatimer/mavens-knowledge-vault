## PRE-READ DOCUMENT (~1,100 words)

**Title:** Tim Latimer — Tiger 21 Portfolio Defense Pre-Read  
**Subtitle:** January 20, 2026 | Calgary Group 3

---

I was seventeen when I learned what community actually means.

The garage burned in the middle of winter. I'd been welding alone — the only boy on a dairy farm with seven sisters, trying to fix stanchions for a barn we couldn't afford to hire anyone to build properly. Something sparked. By the time I understood what was happening, there was nothing to do but watch.

The flames didn't care about the snow. Our farm was called Trail's End because that's what it was — too far down a country road for fire trucks to reach in time. The blaze tore through the modest garage, greedily consuming the family car, a 1962 Pontiac Laurentian. It took the tractor. The workbench. Every tool we owned. Everything that kept a 65-head dairy operation alive.

My parents were away that afternoon. No cavalry coming. I managed to save the attached wooden granary, but the fire took the utility pole feeding electricity to the barn. And there was milking to be done that night.

It was a horrific feeling, just thinking about how disappointed my parents would be.

The ruins smoldered for a month through the dead of winter, smelling of burned farm equipment and mistakes that couldn't be undone. Through the ashes, the Pontiac's sooty taillights stared mockingly at me. You might think nothing was lost that money couldn't replace. But that's the thing: there wasn't any money. That's why I'd been welding alone in the first place.

We never did rebuild the garage. We never replaced the car. But we restored power to the barn that night. The cattle didn't care about our crisis.

A few weeks later, I looked up and saw trucks rolling down our driveway. Ten of them. Each carrying two or three Mennonite men.

Nobody called them.

My grandmother had been Mennonite — fled persecution in Russia, settled near Saskatoon in the early 1900s, spoke Low German and farmed the prairie soil. When my mother later moved to Alberta, she kept in touch with Mennonite families nearby. Somehow, word of our fire had reached them.

These men showed up with nothing but their labour and a quiet instinct to help. They trudged through nearly two feet of snow, crossing the little farm's fields, to collect the square hay bales that had been sitting out since fall. One by one, they hauled them into the barnyard, stacking them in neat rows until the work was done. One less burden.

Then they left.

No invoice. No publicity. No expectation.

---

I didn't understand it then. I was seventeen and embarrassed and grateful and confused all at once. But that moment stayed with me.

Forty years later, I'd built over two hundred stores serving the left-out, left-behind, and working poor. Payday loans. Pawn shops. The businesses polite people don't talk about at dinner parties.

Here's what I learned behind that counter: every customer who walks in is vulnerable, transparent, genuine, and authentic. They're not wearing a mask. They can't afford one. Their story is always some version of the same: the paycheque hasn't arrived in time. The distance between bills and income is just too great.

A real pawn shop has more inventory in the back than out front. The merchandise you see for sale? That's the stuff people didn't come back for. The real business is in the back — hundreds of items on active loans, waiting to be reclaimed. Not merchandise. Savings. Their TV. Their tools. Their grandmother's ring.

I discovered something behind that counter: but for a couple of decisions in my life, I could be standing on the other side of it, asking for the loan. That's not marketing. That's identity.

That perspective is my edge. It's also my responsibility.

---

I'm a cowboy entrepreneur. A guerrilla entrepreneur. A best-practice junkie who built on Peter Drucker's principle: culture eats strategy for breakfast. I'm good up to about 500 employees. Beyond that, I need systems and people I can trust. I've learned where my limits are.

From 1995 to 2005, I built Insta Loan — 101 payday loan stores across Canada. Culture and systems, zeroed in on serving the left-out and left-behind.

Then I did it again. Built Cash Canada — 18 pawn stores — and CashCo — 80 more payday loan locations. Same philosophy, bigger scale. Two hundred stores, built twice, proving the model works.

I've also had three failures that taught me more than those two hundred stores ever could.

**Cash Canada:** Fortress Capital came in as private equity. They gutted the culture. When the numbers didn't hurdle, they sent me a text message — a text — telling me my $10M position was gone.

**CashCo:** Hired a professional CEO to scale. He prioritized process over urgency, spreadsheets over culture. Four years later: $25M down from $60M, losing $5M annually. I'm back running it now.

**B Corp certification:** Five years. Half a million dollars. Rejected — not because of our practices, but because our industry was disqualifying.

Three failures. Three different forces destroying the same thing: purpose.

---

Tomorrow I'm presenting my portfolio and asking for your help. Not validation. Stress-testing.

I've built something called Blue Seal — a "democratized B Corp" where employees and customers vote on whether corporate purpose is authentic. No gatekeepers. No industry exclusions.

I'm also running Maven Village — 392 clients receiving essentials through a $4.29/week subscription. Not charity. Infrastructure for dignity.

Is social impact capitalism marketable? What breaks at scale? What am I not seeing?

I've been doing this in the laboratory of life for four decades. You've built things I haven't. I want to know what you see that I don't.

## LEAVE-BEHIND DOCUMENT: "PROGRESS ISN'T LOUD" (~1,250 words)

**Title:** Progress Isn't Loud  
**Subtitle:** A Maven Village Story

---

Two garbage bags. That's what Nicole carried when she finally left.

She had taken herself and her four-year-old son out of years of abuse. Shelter first. Then transitional housing. Then a place of their own in a new city — no safety net, no family nearby, nothing but what fit in those two bags.

When she reached out to Maven, her words were careful. Almost apologetic. She said she doesn't usually ask for help.

That sentence carried everything. The pride. The fear. The exhaustion of being the one who has to hold it all together.

She wasn't asking to be rescued. She was asking to be considered.

---

The message landed in Sam's inbox on a Tuesday morning.

Sam is what the Maven Village calls its Big Mama — the one who reads the messages that arrive before dawn, the ones written at kitchen tables after the kids are asleep, the ones typed with one thumb while waiting for a bus that's already late.

She doesn't run an office. She runs a village. And every morning, the village tells her what it's carrying.

Some days it's a mother 400 kilometres away from her own mom — a cancer survivor, housebound, climbing two flights of stairs with a cane and groceries under one arm. The daughter used to get phone calls filled with tears. Now she gets peace of mind, knowing someone else is making sure her mother doesn't have to carry toilet paper up those stairs alone.

Some days it's a man named John who works hard, watches every paycheck land and disappear before it clears. He's not asking for luxury. He's asking for breathing room. When someone asked him about his dreams, he paused and said: "If I could, I would go somewhere warm. A beach. I'd feel the sun again." The dream wasn't wealth. The dream was rest.

Some days it's Rose, sick with shingles, rent overdue, writing at her lowest moment: "Im struggling bad today and well tomorrow too… my story wouldnt be uplifting today sorry im wallowing but Im seriously in trouble today."

She apologized for not being inspiring enough while asking for help she wasn't sure she deserved.

That's the damage the system does. It teaches people to apologize for needing anything at all.

---

Maven exists to undo that damage.

If you have a car and it breaks down, you call a tow truck. There's no shame in it. The service exists because everyone understands that cars break down and people need help getting moving again.

But if your life breaks down — if you're choosing between rent and groceries, between medication and the phone bill, between keeping the lights on and keeping your dignity — there's no number to call. No service that shows up without judgment, without paperwork, without making you prove you tried hard enough before you deserve help.

Maven is trying to be that number. The tow truck for your financial life. For your village life.

---

When Nicole learned Maven would help, the first thing she felt was relief.

Not excitement. Not celebration. Relief.

The kind that settles in your chest when someone finally says you don't have to explain yourself anymore. When you don't have to prove you tried hard enough. When no one asks you to justify your pain.

There was disbelief too. Really? Just like that?

No forms. No waiting. No hoops.

When Sam asked if she was safe, Nicole didn't feel processed or managed. She felt seen. Being met where she was. Being trusted. Being treated like a capable mother who had already done the hardest thing possible — leaving — and just needed support to keep going.

She told Sam they were okay. That they had made it to the new city. That they were rebuilding slowly. The focus was simple: safety, stability, staying healthy, staying happy.

And then she said something that captured everything Maven is trying to become.

"It takes a village. Thank you for stepping into mine."

---

Sam keeps that line close. It reminds her why she opens the inbox every morning.

There's a woman named Roxanne who put it differently. When her delivery arrived on the same day she got good news, she wrote back: "You really have my cupboards and my ass. Literally."

Sam laughed when she read it. Then she saved it. Because that's a Maven Moment too — the relief that comes out as humor, the gratitude that doesn't need to be polished to be real.

---

Nicole is still rebuilding. Her son is safe. The two garbage bags have become a small apartment with furniture they picked out together.

She doesn't need Maven forever. That's not the point.

The point is that when she needed someone to step into her village, someone did.

Progress isn't loud. It's felt — in the calm after panic, the laughter after fear, the warmth that returns after a long cold spell.

Two garbage bags. A careful message. A village that answered.

That's a Maven Moment.
