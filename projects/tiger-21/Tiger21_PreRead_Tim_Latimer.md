# Tim Latimer — Tiger 21 Portfolio Defense Pre-Read
**January 20, 2026 | Calgary Group 3**

---

I was seventeen when I learned what community actually means.

The garage burned in the middle of winter. I'd been welding alone—a farm kid with too much confidence and not enough supervision. The spark landed somewhere I didn't see. By the time I smelled smoke, the rafters were catching. I got out. The building didn't.

I stood in the snow and watched my father's Pontiac melt. The taillights glowed red through the flames before the glass shattered.

That was Trail's End Farm, Alberta, around 1974. Mennonite country. A place where neighbors still meant something, though I didn't fully understand what.

A few weeks later, ten trucks rolled down our driveway.

Nobody called them. No one organized a work party. They just showed up—our Mennonite neighbors, their trucks loaded with equipment, ready to haul hay bales and help us recover what we'd lost. They worked all day. When they finished, they left.

No invoice. No publicity. No expectation of return. Not even a proper thank you expected.

I'd never seen anything like it. I've spent forty years trying to build businesses that work the same way.

---

## What I Built

I'm a payday lender. A pawn shop operator. What the industry calls a "predator lender." I've owned and operated over 200 stores across Canada—101 Insta Loan stores in the first wave, then 80 CashCo locations and 18 Cash Canada stores. At peak, about 500 employees.

The customers who walk through those doors are the left out, the left behind, and the working poor. They bring their televisions, their tools, their jewelry. They're always short for something—rent, utilities, a car repair they can't afford to skip. They share their world with you instantly. No pretense. No negotiation. Just the raw facts of a life running behind.

I've spent four decades on my side of that counter. But I've never forgotten: for a couple of decisions, I could be on the other side.

That perspective is my edge. It's also my responsibility.

---

## What Failed

I've had three failures that taught me more than 200 successful stores ever could.

**Cash Canada and Fortress Capital.** I sold 18 stores for $25 million US and carried $10 million in a preferred position with Fortress. I sat on their board. I watched them bring in cost-cutters who gutted the culture that made those stores work. Two years later, I got a text message: my $10 million was gone. "You didn't hurdle." No call. No conversation. Just a text.

Private equity doesn't protect purpose. Lesson learned.

**CashCo and the Professional CEO.** I thought I'd done everything right. Hired a professional CEO to scale from $60 million to $150 million. Put in a board. Proper governance. Everything the business schools teach. Four and a half years later, the company came back to me at $25 million, losing $5 million annually, with the bank foreclosing.

The CEO lacked urgency. Prioritized process over speed. Made decisions by spreadsheet without understanding what the numbers meant in this world. Process without purpose kills speed.

**B Corp Certification.** I spent five years and nearly half a million dollars pursuing B Corp certification for CashCo. Did everything they asked. Rejected—not because of our practices, but because of our industry classification. They decided lending to the working poor couldn't be ethical, regardless of how we did it.

The gatekeepers judged the category, not the behavior.

---

## What I'm Building Now

I'm back as CEO of CashCo. Rebuilding from $12 million outstanding. We have four Pawn Princess locations with plans for five to ten more in 2026. I'm developing something called Blue Seal—a "democratized B Corp" where employees and customers vote on whether corporate purpose is authentic.

No gatekeepers. No industry exclusions. No consultants deciding who's ethical.

I'm also running Maven Village—a subscription service that provides essentials like toilet paper to financially vulnerable Canadians. Not charity. Infrastructure for dignity. A tow truck for your financial life. We have 392 clients currently.

---

## What I'm Asking

I'm presenting this portfolio defense to get your wisdom on one question:

**Is social impact capitalism marketable—or am I off my rocker?**

I've built this way for forty years. Now I've codified it into a system called Blue Seal. I need people who've scaled businesses to tell me what breaks. What safeguards I'm missing. What blind spots come from spending four decades inside this world.

I'll bring three specific questions. I'll also bring Cloud Butterflies—a sheet for you to write down any insight, pushback, or question that strikes you during the session. I'll collect them at the end.

When I was seventeen, those trucks showed up without anyone calling them. That's the world I'm trying to build. I want to know if it can survive contact with capital markets.
