# The Jolene Protocol
## A Complete Framework for Grade-A Narrative Journalism

**Origin:** Derived from analysis of the work of Jolene Latimer, Emmy-winning journalist, whose feature journalism demonstrates mastery of narrative craft across multiple genres (family memoir, sports grief, redemption arc, character study).

**Reference Work:** "Our Modern Nativity" (Canadian Mennonite, December 2025) — the foundational text from which these principles were extracted.

---

## Part 1: What Makes an A-Grade Story

### The Opening Hook

**Standard:** First sentence does the work of a paragraph. Drops reader into the moment with immediate tension. No throat-clearing.

**Evidence from Jolene's work:**
- "Because there was nothing else he could do, he had no choice but to watch it burn."
- Immediate tension, no preamble, you're in the scene before you know what's happening

**Test:** If you can cut your first paragraph and lose nothing, your hook is too late.

### Concrete Detail

**Standard:** Specific, visual, emotionally loaded without being sentimental. A trained eye finds the image that holds everything.

**Evidence:**
- "The 1962 Pontiac Laurentian's sooty taillights stared mockingly"
- Not "a car" — a specific car, a specific detail, doing emotional work

**Test:** Could another writer have chosen this same detail? If yes, dig deeper.

### The Physical Object That Holds Meaning

**Standard:** Every Jolene story has one object that carries the weight of the theme. This is her signature move.

**Examples from her work:**
- Sooty taillights (Our Modern Nativity)
- Hay bales (Our Modern Nativity)
- The alumni blazer (profile work)
- The penalty box (sports journalism)

**Application:**
- Introduce the object early (often in the hook)
- Let it do emotional work silently through the piece
- Call it back in the close

**Test:** What physical thing could you remove from your story that would cause it to collapse? That's your object.

### Structural Sophistication

**Standard:** Thematic structure, not chronological. Frame narratives are permitted when they earn their complexity.

**Evidence:**
- In "Our Modern Nativity," Jolene's own experience at Menno House in Manhattan becomes the lens for discovering her father's story
- Two timelines, one theme, neither fighting for dominance

**Jolene's architecture:** She knows where she's going before she starts writing. Each piece has visible bones.

### Restraint

**Standard:** Trust the material more than the reader. The temptation to overwrite, to sentimentalize, is enormous. Resist.

**Evidence:**
- Writing about her own family — the trap of overwriting is massive
- She doesn't fall in
- The suffering is implied through action, not description

**What restraint looks like:**
- "Years of abuse" appears once, never elaborated
- No catalog of suffering
- Violence implied through what fits in two garbage bags, not described

**Test:** Are you explaining because the reader needs it, or because you're afraid they won't feel it? If the latter, cut.

### Pacing

**Standard:** Hold back the key moment until maximum impact. Build isolation first, then let grace arrive.

**Evidence:**
- The Mennonite trucks don't arrive until you've felt the full weight of standing alone watching everything burn
- The relief is earned by the deprivation that precedes it

**Principle:** Make the reader wait for the thing they came for. The delay is the craft.

### Quote Discipline

**Standard:** One great quote per source. No piling.

**Evidence:**
- Each voice in her pieces gets one line
- The quotes she chooses are the ones the subject handed her — not constructed, not elicited through leading questions

**Test:** If you have three quotes from one source, two of them are weaker than the one you should keep.

### The Kicker

**Standard:** End on a line the subject hands you. Simple. Final. Knows when to stop.

**Evidence:**
- "It was the first time I ever saw someone give without expecting anything in return."
- No elaboration after, no "and that's why..." — just the line, then silence

**Structure:** The kicker often calls back to the opening. The piece becomes a closed loop.

**Test:** Read your last three sentences aloud. If you can cut two, do it.

## Part 2: Structural Principles

### The Three-Act Arc

Every Jolene story follows one of two patterns:

**Pattern A: Loss / Pain / Redemption**
- Act 1: Establish what was lost or what is at stake
- Act 2: Sit in the difficulty (don't rush this)
- Act 3: The turn — grace, resolution, or earned insight

**Pattern B: Tension / Revelation / Resolution**
- Act 1: Present the surface tension
- Act 2: Reveal what's underneath
- Act 3: Show how knowing changes everything

### Scene Over Summary

**Principle:** Build scenes, don't summarize them.

**Wrong:** "She had been through a lot."
**Right:** "Two garbage bags. That's what Nicole carried when she finally left."

Scenes have:
- Specific time ("Tuesday morning")
- Specific place ("kitchen table after the kids are asleep")
- Specific action (what someone did, not what they felt)
- Specific sensory detail (what you'd see if you were there)

### The Turn

**Placement:** Approximately 60% through the piece.

**What it is:** The moment where everything shifts. Not the climax — the hinge.

**Examples:**
- "And then Maven said yes."
- The trucks appearing on the horizon
- The quote that reframes everything that came before

### Callback Structure

**Principle:** The close echoes the open. The piece becomes a closed loop.

**Example:**
- Open: "Two garbage bags."
- Close: "Two garbage bags. A careful message. A village that answered."

This isn't repetition — it's return with new meaning.

## Part 3: Voice Principles

### Warm But Not Soft

The voice has affection for its subjects but doesn't protect them from complexity. Warmth is not sentimentality.

### Trust the Material More Than the Reader

Don't explain what the story already shows. If the scene works, the reader will feel it.

### Restraint With Sentiment

Let the story do the emotional work. The writer's job is to arrange the evidence, not to tell the reader what to feel.

### Thematic Ambition

**Not:** "A profile of a person"
**But:** "An essay about an idea that happens to have a person in it"

Every Jolene story is secretly about something larger:
- Grief
- Belonging
- Loyalty
- Grace
- Dignity

The subject is the vehicle. The theme is the destination.

## Part 4: What Jolene Doesn't Do

### Never Overexplain Transitions

**Wrong:** "That's where Luce sees the strongest connection between..."
**Right:** [Just move to the next scene. The reader will follow.]

### Never Sentimentalize

**Wrong:** "It was a beautiful moment of human connection."
**Right:** [Describe the moment. Let the reader decide it was beautiful.]

### Never Let the Piece Run Long in the Middle

The middle is where stories go to die. Trim ruthlessly. If you're bored, the reader left ten minutes ago.

### Never State the Theme

**Wrong:** "This is a story about dignity."
**Right:** [Show dignity in action. Never name it unless a subject names it first.]

### Never Pile Quotes

One great quote per source. If you have more, save them for another piece.

## Part 5: Adaptation for Length

### At 1,200-1,400 Words (Standard Jolene Length)

You have room for:
- Frame narrative (two timelines)
- Multiple voices (3-5 sources)
- Full three-act arc with breathing room
- Scene development
- Building suspense (delay the key moment)

### At 500 Words (Compressed Form)

**What Gets Preserved:**
| Element | Adaptation |
|---------|------------|
| Opening hook | Non-negotiable. First sentence does the work of a paragraph. |
| Physical object | One object. Possibly introduced in the hook itself. |
| Restraint | More critical than ever — no room for even one wasted word |
| Kicker | Must land. Final sentence carries more weight at this length. |
| Theme earned | Still possible, but through implication, not development |

**What Gets Sacrificed:**
| Element | Why It Won't Fit |
|---------|------------------|
| Frame narrative | No room for two timelines or two perspectives |
| Multiple voices | One protagonist, maybe one supporting quote maximum |
| Full three-act arc | Compressed to tension → turn → resolution |
| Building suspense | No space to delay the key moment |
| Scene development | One scene, maybe a scene-and-a-half |

**Structural Model for 500 Words:**
| Section | Word Budget | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Hook + scene entry | 75-100 | Drop reader into the moment with object |
| Context/tension | 150-175 | Who, what's at stake, why it matters |
| Turn/revelation | 125-150 | The thing that changes everything |
| Resolution + kicker | 50-75 | Close with the line that stays |

**The Key Difference:**
- At 1,200 words: Jolene builds toward the moment
- At 500 words: She captures the moment

Think of it as the difference between a short story and a prose poem. Both can be A-grade. Both require mastery. But they're different disciplines.

## Part 6: Grading Criteria

### A Grade Requirements

| Element | Standard |
|---------|----------|
| Opening hook | Immediate tension, image first, no throat-clearing |
| Physical object | Present, introduced early, called back at close |
| Quote discipline | One quote per source, no piling |
| Restraint | No sentimentality, no over-explanation |
| Scene over summary | Stays in the moment throughout |
| Theme earned | Shown, never stated (until it arrives naturally through subject's words) |
| Kicker | Callback structure, knows when to stop |
| No marketing language | Ends on the story, not an ask |

### A- Grade (Fixable Flaws)

Common issues that drop a piece from A to A-:

1. **Naming the theme explicitly** — "Then something else arrived quietly. Dignity." (Let the reader supply the word)

2. **Manifesto language intruding** — "That's what it looks like when someone gets caught instead of ignored" (This is the writer's frame, not the subject's experience)

3. **Generalizations** — "the way most people do" (Lifts reader out of specific story into marketing language)

4. **Over-explaining transitions** — Trusting readers less than the material warrants

### B Grade Issues

- Multiple weak quotes where one strong one would do
- Theme stated before it's earned
- Middle section bloated
- Closing with an ask or marketing language
- Opening that clears its throat before getting to the story

## Part 7: Application Checklist

Before submitting a piece, verify:

**Structure:**
- [ ] Hook drops reader into scene immediately
- [ ] Physical object introduced in first 100 words
- [ ] Turn occurs at approximately 60% mark
- [ ] Close calls back to open
- [ ] No section runs long in the middle

**Voice:**
- [ ] No sentimentality — material trusted to carry emotion
- [ ] No over-explanation — transitions are clean
- [ ] Theme earned through scene, not stated
- [ ] Quotes are earned, not piled

**Discipline:**
- [ ] One quote per source maximum
- [ ] No marketing language
- [ ] No italicized "lesson" paragraphs at the end
- [ ] Final sentence is the real ending (nothing after)

**Final Test:**
- Read the last three sentences aloud. Can you cut two? Do it.
- Read the first paragraph. Can you start later? Do it.
- Remove every adjective. Add back only the essential ones.

## Part 8: The Emotional Anchor

Jolene doesn't write profiles of people.

She writes essays about ideas that happen to have people in them.

The subjects are vehicles. The themes are destinations:
- Grief
- Belonging
- Loyalty
- Grace
- Dignity
- The invisible labor of care
- What it means to be seen

Every piece answers one question the reader didn't know they were asking.
