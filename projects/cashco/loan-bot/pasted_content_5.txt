The 10 Questions

 

1.The Unforgettable Client Story: Think about a client you’ll never forget. What made that relationship so special or memorable? Can you share a brief story?

One client I will always remember is Lori. She came to my branch for the first time after not being helped at another location near her home because the staff was new and there were system issues. She walked in the branch at closing time. I stayed late to make sure she was helped and didn’t leave disappointed. That one decision made a lasting impact. For the next five years that I worked at that branch, she stayed loyal and always came to me for her loan, even when the branch later became digital. She trusted me, waited for me specifically, and that relationship started simply because I chose to take the extra time to help her when she needed it most.

 
2.The Toughest Conversation: When a client calls in distress because they can't make a payment, what are the first words you say, and what is your primary goal in that conversation (beyond collecting the payment)?

“I understand — life happens. Let’s work through this together.” My goal was to make them feel supported and calm. Once they feel heard, we used to work toward a solution that actually makes sense for them.

 

3.Beyond the Loan: What are the most common topics you talk about with clients that have nothing to do with their loan or finances? (e.g., family, hobbies, work, their community).
I often talk with clients about their work, their family, or what’s going on in their lives. If I knew their child had a birthday or something important coming up, I’d ask how it went the next time we spoke.

 4.Celebrating the Wins: How do you celebrate a client's success, whether it’s paying off their loan or just making a few on-time payments in a row? What does that sound like?
When a client ( who was struggling ) paid off a loan or stayed consistent with payments, especially with flex loans, I always made sure to acknowledge it.

 

5.Your Secret Weapon: What is one small, simple thing you do that seems to have the biggest positive impact on how a client feels? (e.g., remembering a pet’s name, asking about a recent trip).
My biggest strength was remembering details from previous conversations. If a client mentioned something important the month before, I’d follow up the next time we spoke. That showed them I genuinely cared 

 

6.The First Five Minutes: When you talk to a new client for the first time, what is the most important thing you do to build a connection and earn their trust right away?
When speaking to a new client, I always started by asking how they heard about us. That helped me understand what they already knew and whether they were referred to by someone. From there, I focused on explaining how we support our clients and how we’re there to help and not judge. This really built a great connection.

 

7.The "Not Yet" Moment: If you have to tell someone they aren't approved for a loan, how do you handle that conversation to ensure they still feel respected and hopeful, not rejected?
If a client wasn’t approved, I was always honest but kind. I explained the reason clearly and let them know it wasn’t permanent. I made sure to tell them what they could work on and encouraged them to come back after 30 days.

 

8.The Community Connector: Have you ever suggested a non-financial resource to a client (e.g., a community event, a local charity, a job fair)? What was the situation?
Because my branch was in a low-income area, I often shared information about local resources — shelters, food services, job fairs, and other community support options.

 

9.The Power of Remembering: What are 2-3 types of personal details you try to remember about your long-term clients? How do you use that information to strengthen the relationship?
I used to remember their family or work situation or any hardships
 

10.If You Had a Superpower...: Imagine you had a perfect digital assistant (our Maven + Cashco Guardian!) that could help you care for your clients. What are three things you would have it do for them every day or every week to make their lives better?
I would send then reminders about their payments, and ask them how life is going, remember their birthdays to wish them
